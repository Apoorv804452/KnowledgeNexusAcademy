üîπ 1. What are life processes?

To be alive, an organism must perform certain maintenance functions. Even when resting or sleeping, the body needs to repair damage and maintain order. These functions are called life processes.

  • Nutrition ‚Äì obtaining food and energy.
  • Respiration ‚Äì breaking down food to release energy.
  • Transportation ‚Äì distributing substances throughout the body.
  • Excretion ‚Äì removing metabolic wastes.

In single‚Äëcelled organisms, diffusion across the cell surface is enough. But in multicellular organisms, specialized organs are needed because diffusion is too slow to reach all cells.

üìò In‚Äëtext questions

Q1. Why is diffusion insufficient to meet the oxygen requirements of multicellular organisms like humans?
Ans: In multicellular organisms, all cells are not in direct contact with the environment. Diffusion is a slow process and cannot transport oxygen to distant cells quickly enough.

Q2. What criteria do we use to decide whether something is alive?
Ans: Visible movement, growth, response to stimuli, and the presence of molecular movements (life processes like nutrition, respiration) indicate life.

Q3. What are outside raw materials used for by an organism?
Ans: They are used for energy (to power life processes) and for building materials (growth, repair).

Q4. What processes would you consider essential for maintaining life?
Ans: Nutrition, respiration, transportation, excretion.

üîπ 2. Nutrition
2.1 Autotrophic nutrition

Organisms that make their own food from inorganic sources (CO‚ÇÇ, water) using light energy are autotrophs (green plants, some bacteria).

Photosynthesis equation:

6CO‚ÇÇ + 12H‚ÇÇO ‚Äî(sunlight, chlorophyll)‚Üí C‚ÇÜH‚ÇÅ‚ÇÇO‚ÇÜ + 6O‚ÇÇ + 6H‚ÇÇO

Events of photosynthesis (three steps):

  1. Absorption of light energy by chlorophyll (in chloroplasts).
  2. Conversion of light energy to chemical energy and splitting of water into hydrogen and oxygen (photolysis).
  3. Reduction of CO‚ÇÇ to carbohydrates (glucose).

Raw materials and their sources:

  • CO‚ÇÇ ‚Äì enters leaf through stomata (pores on leaf surface).
  • Water ‚Äì absorbed by roots from soil.
  • Sunlight ‚Äì captured by chlorophyll.
  • Minerals (nitrogen, phosphorus, etc.) ‚Äì taken up from soil by roots; nitrogen in form of nitrates/nitrites or organic compounds from bacteria.

Stomata and guard cells: Stomata are tiny pores surrounded by guard cells. When guard cells absorb water, they swell and open the pore; when they lose water, they shrink and close the pore. This regulates gas exchange and water loss.

Activity 5.1 (Chlorophyll essential): Take a variegated leaf plant (e.g., money plant). Keep in dark for 3 days to use up starch. Then place in sunlight for 6 hours. Pluck a leaf, trace green areas. Boil leaf in water, then in alcohol (to remove chlorophyll). Dip in iodine solution. Only green parts turn blue‚Äëblack, proving chlorophyll is necessary for photosynthesis.
Activity 5.2 (CO‚ÇÇ essential): Take two potted plants. Keep in dark for 3 days. Place each on a glass plate. Near one plant, keep a watch‚Äëglass with potassium hydroxide (absorbs CO‚ÇÇ). Cover both with bell jars and seal. Keep in sunlight for 2 hours. Test leaves for starch. The plant without CO‚ÇÇ (with KOH) shows no starch ‚Äì CO‚ÇÇ is essential.
2.2 Heterotrophic nutrition

Organisms that cannot make their own food and depend on others are heterotrophs. Types:

  • Holozoic nutrition: ingestion of whole food and internal digestion (humans, animals).
  • Saprophytic nutrition: feeding on dead/decaying matter (fungi, bread mould). Enzymes are secreted outside to digest food, then absorbed.
  • Parasitic nutrition: living on or inside a host and deriving food from it (cuscuta, ticks, leeches, tapeworms).
2.3 How do organisms obtain their nutrition? (unicellular examples)

Amoeba: Uses pseudopodia (false feet) to engulf food ‚Äì forms a food vacuole. Digestive enzymes break down food, nutrients diffuse into cytoplasm. Undigested residue is expelled.

Paramecium: Has a definite shape with cilia covering the surface. Cilia move food into a specific spot (oral groove) where it is ingested.

2.4 Nutrition in human beings ‚Äì the digestive system

The alimentary canal is a long tube from mouth to anus, with different regions specialized for digestion.

Mouth

  • Teeth: cut, grind, and crush food into smaller pieces.
  • Tongue: helps in mixing food with saliva and moving it toward the pharynx.
  • Saliva: secreted by salivary glands. Contains salivary amylase (breaks down starch into sugar) and mucus (moistens food, makes it slippery).
Activity 5.3 (Saliva digests starch): Take 1% starch solution in two test tubes (A and B). Add 1 mL saliva to A, leave both for 20‚Äì30 min. Add iodine solution. A shows no blue colour (starch digested), B turns blue. Proves saliva breaks starch.

Oesophagus (food pipe)

Muscles in the wall contract rhythmically ‚Äì peristalsis ‚Äì to push food down to stomach.

Stomach

A large, muscular organ. Gastric glands in its wall secrete:

  • Hydrochloric acid (HCl): creates acidic medium, kills bacteria.
  • Pepsin (enzyme): digests proteins into peptides.
  • Mucus: protects the stomach lining from acid.

Food is churned into acidic semi‚Äëfluid called chyme.

Small intestine

Longest part, highly coiled. Receives secretions from liver and pancreas.

  • Liver: produces bile (stored in gall bladder). Bile is alkaline, neutralizes acid, and emulsifies fats (breaks large fat globules into smaller droplets) ‚Äì increases surface area for enzyme action.
  • Pancreas: secretes pancreatic juice containing:
    • Trypsin: digests proteins.
    • Lipase: breaks down emulsified fats into fatty acids and glycerol.
  • Intestinal juice (succus entericus): secreted by glands in intestinal wall. Contains many enzymes:
    • Maltase, lactase, sucrase ‚Äì break down disaccharides into monosaccharides (glucose, etc.).
    • Peptidases ‚Äì break peptides into amino acids.
    • Lipase (small amount) ‚Äì further fat digestion.

After complete digestion, the inner lining of the small intestine has millions of villi (finger‚Äëlike projections). Each villus contains blood capillaries and a lacteal (lymph vessel). Villi greatly increase surface area for absorption. Nutrients (amino acids, glucose, fatty acids, glycerol) enter blood/lymph and are transported to all cells.

Large intestine

Absorbs water and some salts from undigested material. The remaining semi‚Äësolid waste (faeces) passes to rectum and is expelled through anus (regulated by anal sphincter).

Dental caries: Bacteria in mouth act on sugars to produce acids that soften enamel. Plaque (bacterial film) covers teeth, preventing saliva from neutralizing acid. Brushing after meals removes plaque.

üìò In‚Äëtext questions

Q1. What are the differences between autotrophic nutrition and heterotrophic nutrition?
Ans: Autotrophs make their own food using light/CO‚ÇÇ (plants). Heterotrophs depend on others for food (animals, fungi).

Q2. Where do plants get each of the raw materials required for photosynthesis?
Ans: CO‚ÇÇ from air through stomata; water from soil via roots; sunlight; chlorophyll in chloroplasts.

Q3. What is the role of the acid in our stomach?
Ans: HCl creates acidic medium for pepsin to work and kills bacteria.

Q4. What is the function of digestive enzymes?
Ans: They break down complex insoluble food into simple soluble molecules that can be absorbed.

Q5. How is the small intestine designed to absorb digested food?
Ans: It is long, with villi (finger‚Äëlike projections) that increase surface area and have rich blood supply.

üîπ 3. Respiration

Respiration is the process of breaking down food (glucose) to release energy, which is stored as ATP.

3.1 Breakdown of glucose ‚Äì different pathways

The first step (common to all) occurs in cytoplasm: glucose (6‚Äëcarbon) ‚Üí pyruvate (3‚Äëcarbon). Then:

  • Aerobic respiration (in mitochondria): pyruvate + O‚ÇÇ ‚Üí CO‚ÇÇ + H‚ÇÇO + 36‚Äì38 ATP.
  • Anaerobic respiration in yeast: pyruvate ‚Üí ethanol + CO‚ÇÇ + 2 ATP (fermentation).
  • Anaerobic in muscle cells (oxygen shortage): pyruvate ‚Üí lactic acid + 2 ATP. Lactic acid accumulation causes muscle cramps.
Activity 5.4 (Exhaled air has more CO‚ÇÇ): Take lime water in two test tubes. Blow air into one (exhaled air) ‚Äì turns milky quickly. Pass normal air into other using syringe ‚Äì takes longer. Proves exhaled air contains more CO‚ÇÇ.
Activity 5.5 (Yeast produces CO‚ÇÇ): Take fruit juice/sugar solution + yeast in a test tube. Fit with cork and bent tube dipping into lime water. After some time, lime water turns milky ‚Äì CO‚ÇÇ from fermentation.

ATP ‚Äì the energy currency

ADP + phosphate + energy (from respiration) ‚Üí ATP. When ATP breaks into ADP and phosphate, energy is released for cellular work (contraction, protein synthesis, nerve impulses, etc.).

3.2 Human respiratory system

Air enters through nostrils (hairs and mucus filter dust). Passes through pharynx ‚Üí larynx ‚Üí trachea (supported by cartilage rings to prevent collapse). Trachea divides into bronchi ‚Üí bronchioles ‚Üí alveoli.

Alveoli are tiny air sacs with extremely thin walls and a rich network of blood capillaries. They provide a huge surface area for gas exchange. Oxygen diffuses into blood, CO‚ÇÇ diffuses out.

Breathing rate: Terrestrial organisms breathe slower because air has high O‚ÇÇ concentration; aquatic animals (like fish) breathe faster because dissolved O‚ÇÇ is low. Fish take in water through mouth and force it over gills where O‚ÇÇ is absorbed.

Activity 5.6 (Fish breathing): Observe fish in aquarium. Mouth and gill covers open and close in coordination. Count mouth movements per minute ‚Äì much faster than human breathing rate.
3.3 Transport of gases

Oxygen is carried by haemoglobin in red blood cells (as oxyhaemoglobin). Carbon dioxide is transported mainly as bicarbonate in plasma and also dissolved.

üìò In‚Äëtext questions

Q1. What advantage over an aquatic organism does a terrestrial organism have with regard to obtaining oxygen for respiration?
Ans: Air contains much more oxygen than water, so terrestrial organisms can obtain oxygen more easily and breathe slower.

Q2. What are the different ways in which glucose is oxidised to provide energy in various organisms?
Ans: Aerobic (with O‚ÇÇ) ‚Üí CO‚ÇÇ + H‚ÇÇO + 38 ATP; anaerobic (without O‚ÇÇ) ‚Üí ethanol + CO‚ÇÇ (yeast) or lactic acid (muscle) + 2 ATP.

Q3. How is oxygen and carbon dioxide transported in human beings?
Ans: O‚ÇÇ bound to haemoglobin in RBCs; CO‚ÇÇ as bicarbonate in plasma.

Q4. How are the lungs designed in human beings to maximise the area for exchange of gases?
Ans: Millions of alveoli provide huge surface area; thin walls and rich capillaries ensure efficient diffusion.

üîπ 4. Transportation
4.1 Transportation in human beings

Components: Heart (pump), blood vessels (arteries, veins, capillaries), blood.

Heart (double circulation): Four‚Äëchambered (two atria, two ventricles).

  1. Deoxygenated blood from body ‚Üí right atrium ‚Üí right ventricle ‚Üí lungs (pulmonary circulation).
  2. Oxygenated blood from lungs ‚Üí left atrium ‚Üí left ventricle ‚Üí body (systemic circulation).

Valves prevent backflow. Separation of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood ensures efficient oxygen supply ‚Äì essential for warm‚Äëblooded animals (birds, mammals) that need constant energy to maintain body temperature.

Blood vessels:

  • Arteries: thick, elastic walls; carry blood away from heart under high pressure.
  • Veins: thinner, have valves to prevent backflow; carry blood toward heart.
  • Capillaries: one‚Äëcell thick walls; site of exchange of materials (O‚ÇÇ, CO‚ÇÇ, nutrients, wastes).

Blood pressure: Force exerted by blood on artery walls. Systolic (during contraction) ~120 mm Hg, diastolic (relaxation) ~80 mm Hg. Measured with sphygmomanometer. High blood pressure (hypertension) can damage arteries.

Platelets: Circulating cell fragments that help clot blood at injury sites, preventing leaks.

Lymph: Some plasma, proteins, and blood cells escape from capillaries into intercellular spaces ‚Äì forms tissue fluid (lymph). Lymph carries digested fats, drains excess fluid, and returns to blood via lymphatic vessels.

4.2 Transportation in plants

Plants have low energy needs, so transport systems are slower. Two main conducting tissues:

  • Xylem: transports water and minerals from roots upward. Consists of vessels and tracheids. Movement is driven by transpiration pull (evaporation from leaves creates suction) and root pressure (at night).
  • Phloem: transports products of photosynthesis (sucrose, amino acids) from leaves to other parts. Translocation occurs in sieve tubes with help of companion cells, using ATP. Movement can be upward or downward depending on plant's needs (e.g., sugars to roots in winter, to buds in spring).
Activity 5.8 (Transpiration): Take two pots ‚Äì one with plant, one with stick. Cover soil with plastic. Place in sunlight. Water droplets appear inside the cover over the plant (transpiration), but not over the stick.

üìò In‚Äëtext questions

Q1. What are the components of the transport system in human beings? What are the functions of these components?
Ans: Heart (pump), arteries (carry blood away), veins (bring blood back), capillaries (exchange), blood (carries O‚ÇÇ, food, wastes).

Q2. Why is it necessary to separate oxygenated and deoxygenated blood in mammals and birds?
Ans: To maintain high oxygen supply for constant body temperature (warm‚Äëblooded animals).

Q3. What are the components of the transport system in highly organised plants?
Ans: Xylem (water & minerals) and phloem (food).

Q4. How are water and minerals transported in plants?
Ans: Through xylem by transpiration pull and root pressure.

Q5. How is food transported in plants?
Ans: Through phloem using energy (ATP) ‚Äì pressure difference moves sap.

üîπ 5. Excretion
5.1 Excretion in human beings

Excretory system: Kidneys (pair), ureters, urinary bladder, urethra.

Kidney: Located in abdomen, one on each side of backbone. They filter blood to remove nitrogenous wastes (urea, uric acid) and excess salts/water.

Nephron ‚Äì functional unit of kidney (about 1 million per kidney).

  • Glomerulus: cluster of capillaries inside Bowman's capsule. Blood pressure forces water, urea, glucose, salts into Bowman's capsule ‚Äì this is ultrafiltration.
  • Bowman's capsule: cup‚Äëshaped structure that collects filtrate.
  • Tubule: long, coiled tube where reabsorption occurs ‚Äì useful substances (glucose, amino acids, most water, salts) are reabsorbed into blood capillaries.
  • Secretion: additional wastes (H‚Å∫, K‚Å∫) are secreted into tubule.
  • Final fluid (urine) flows through collecting duct ‚Üí ureter ‚Üí bladder ‚Üí urethra.

Regulation of urine: The amount of water reabsorbed depends on body's water needs, controlled by hormone ADH (vasopressin). If water is low, more water is reabsorbed; if excess, less reabsorption.

Artificial kidney (haemodialysis): Used when kidneys fail. Blood is passed through tubes with semi‚Äëpermeable lining, bathed in dialysing fluid (same composition as blood except no wastes). Waste diffuses out; clean blood returned. No reabsorption occurs.

5.2 Excretion in plants

Plants produce less nitrogenous waste. Methods:

  • Oxygen (waste of photosynthesis) diffuses out through stomata.
  • CO‚ÇÇ (waste of respiration) diffuses out at night.
  • Excess water removed by transpiration.
  • Wastes stored in vacuoles, old leaves (shed), or as resins and gums in old xylem.
  • Some wastes are excreted into the soil.

üìò In‚Äëtext questions

Q1. Describe the structure and functioning of nephrons.
Ans: Nephron has Bowman's capsule, glomerulus, tubule. Blood filtered in glomerulus, reabsorption of useful substances along tubule, secretion of extra wastes, finally urine is formed.

Q2. What are the methods used by plants to get rid of excretory products?
Ans: Diffusion (O‚ÇÇ, CO‚ÇÇ), transpiration (water), storage in vacuoles/old leaves/resins, shedding leaves.

Q3. How is the amount of urine produced regulated?
Ans: By reabsorption of water in nephron, controlled by hormones (ADH) according to body's water needs.
